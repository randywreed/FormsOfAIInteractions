INSIDE THE MODEL: 3D NEURAL NETWORK VISUALIZATION
A Plain-English Guide for Non-Technical Viewers
=================================================

WHAT YOU'RE WATCHING
--------------------
You're looking inside a neural network — the kind of "brain" that powers
modern AI. Instead of showing you abstract diagrams or equations, this
visualization represents the network as a 3D matrix of numbers floating
in space. Every number you see is a weight, activation, or data point
flowing through the model.

Think of it like watching the Matrix, but these numbers have meaning:
they're the actual computations happening inside an AI model as it
processes information.


THE BIG IDEA (30-SECOND VERSION)
---------------------------------
Neural networks are just giant collections of numbers. When you feed an
AI model some input (like a sentence or an image), it doesn't "think"
in the way humans do. Instead, it performs millions of mathematical
operations on arrays of numbers.

What you're seeing is a stylized representation of those numbers. Each
digit (0-9) represents a value somewhere in the network — maybe a weight
that gets multiplied by an input, or an activation that flows from one
layer to the next.

The numbers change continuously because:
  1. Real neural networks constantly update their values during training.
  2. Data flows through them in waves as new inputs are processed.
  3. This visualization randomly updates numbers to simulate that dynamic
     behavior.


WHAT YOU'RE SEEING
------------------

The Matrix (Zoomed Out View)
  When you first load the visualization, you see a 10×10×10 cube of
  numbers — that's 1,000 individual digits total. They're arranged in
  three dimensions:
    • Width (X axis) — columns going left to right
    • Height (Y axis) — rows going up to down  
    • Depth (Z axis) — layers going front to back

  The cube is green-on-black (like classic terminal displays or the
  Matrix movie) to evoke the feeling of looking at raw computer data.

  The numbers flicker and change randomly. About 10% of the digits
  update every 200 milliseconds, simulating the continuous activity
  inside a real neural network.

Inside the Matrix (Zoomed In View)
  Click anywhere on the screen to zoom in. Suddenly you're inside the
  cube, surrounded by floating numbers.

  As you zoom in, three things happen:
    1. The camera moves closer to the center of the cube.
    2. The numbers spread out so you can see the 3D structure.
    3. Your mouse movement now controls the view — move your mouse to
       rotate the entire matrix around you.

  Numbers closer to the camera appear larger and brighter. Numbers far
  away shrink and fade, giving you a sense of depth. This is called
  "perspective projection" — the same technique used in 3D video games
  and movies.

  Click again to zoom back out.


THE CONTROLS
------------
This visualization has only one control: click anywhere to toggle
between zoomed-out and zoomed-in views.

When zoomed in, move your mouse around the screen to rotate the matrix:
  • Move your mouse left/right → rotates the cube horizontally
  • Move your mouse up/down → rotates the cube vertically

Try moving your mouse in a circle to see the cube spin. The rotation is
smooth and follows your cursor in real time.


WHY THIS REPRESENTATION?
-------------------------
You might be wondering: "Why show numbers as floating digits? What do
they represent?"

In a real neural network, every computation boils down to numbers:

  • Weights: Numbers that determine how strongly one neuron connects to
    another. These are learned during training. A weight of 0 means "no
    connection." A large positive weight means "strong connection." A
    negative weight means "inhibitory connection."

  • Activations: Numbers that flow through the network when you give it
    an input. If you feed an image into an image classifier, the raw
    pixel values (0-255) get transformed into activations as they flow
    through each layer.

  • Gradients: During training, the network calculates how much each
    weight should change to improve performance. These are also numbers,
    and they flow backward through the network (backpropagation).

This visualization doesn't distinguish between these types — it just
shows you the essence of what a neural network is: a giant,
multidimensional array of numbers that get updated and transformed
constantly.


THE 3D STRUCTURE
----------------
Why are the numbers arranged in a 3D cube?

In reality, neural network parameters can have many dimensions — not
just three. A typical language model might have tensors with shapes like:

  • (768, 768) — A 2D matrix for a weight layer
  • (12, 64, 768) — A 3D tensor for multi-head attention
  • (50257, 768) — A huge 2D embedding table

But visualizing more than three dimensions is hard (our brains are wired
for 3D space). So this visualization uses a 3D cube as a metaphor for
the multidimensional structure of a neural network.

Think of each "layer" (slice along the Z axis) as representing one layer
of the network:
  • Front layers (small Z values) might represent early processing.
  • Middle layers represent intermediate features.
  • Back layers represent high-level abstractions.

As you zoom in and rotate the cube, you can see how these layers stack
on top of each other, like a sandwich of computation.


WHAT THE NUMBERS MEAN (CONCEPTUALLY)
-------------------------------------
Each digit (0-9) you see is drawn randomly, but in a real neural
network, these numbers would have specific meanings:

  • 0-1: Small positive values. Might represent probabilities or
         normalized activations.

  • 2-5: Medium values. Could be intermediate activations or weights.

  • 6-9: Larger values. Might represent strong signals or important
         features.

In reality, neural networks use floating-point numbers (like 0.7253 or
-1.429), not just single digits. But showing full decimal numbers would
clutter the visualization. Single digits give you the flavor of what's
happening without overwhelming detail.

The key insight is this: everything in a neural network is ultimately
just numbers being added, multiplied, and transformed. There's no magic.
When GPT writes a sentence or Stable Diffusion draws a picture, it's
doing billions of arithmetic operations on numbers like the ones you see
floating in this cube.


WHAT TO TRY
-----------
Here are some things to explore while using the visualization:

  1. Watch the numbers flicker for a while before zooming in. Notice how
     about 10% of them change each time. This simulates the constant
     activity in a real network.

  2. Click to zoom in, then move your mouse slowly in a circle. Watch
     how the entire matrix rotates smoothly. This helps you see the 3D
     structure.

  3. While zoomed in, hold your mouse still for a moment. Notice that
     numbers near the center are largest and brightest, while numbers at
     the edges fade away. This mimics how 3D graphics work.

  4. Click to zoom out, then click in again. Each time you zoom in, the
     perspective resets, and you're looking straight at the cube before
     you start rotating with your mouse.

  5. Think about scale. This cube has 1,000 numbers (10×10×10). A real
     language model like GPT-3 has 175 billion parameters. If each number
     in this visualization represented one billion parameters, you'd need
     175 of these cubes to show GPT-3. And that's just the weights —
     activations during inference would add even more.


WHY THIS MATTERS
----------------
This visualization helps you develop an intuition for what's inside an
AI model. When you hear phrases like:

  • "The model has 7 billion parameters"
     → Picture billions of these numbers, arranged in layers.

  • "Training updates the weights"
     → Picture these numbers flickering and changing as the model learns.

  • "The network has 32 layers"
     → Picture 32 slices of this cube, stacked front to back.

  • "Activations flow forward, gradients flow backward"
     → Picture numbers propagating through the depth of the cube in both
       directions.

Neural networks are often described as "black boxes" because their
internal workings are hard to interpret. This visualization doesn't
solve that problem (interpreting what specific weights mean is still
cutting-edge research), but it does demystify the basic structure: it's
numbers, all the way down.

THE MATRIX AESTHETIC
--------------------
The green-on-black color scheme is a deliberate choice. It references:

  1. The Matrix movies, where streams of green characters represent the
     underlying code of the simulated reality.

  2. Classic computer terminals (VT100, DOS, early Unix systems), where
     green phosphor screens were the norm.

  3. "Hacker culture" aesthetics, where green monospace text signifies
     raw, unfiltered access to a system.

In all of these contexts, the green numbers suggest you're looking at
something fundamental — the lowest level of how the system works, below
the user-friendly interface.

That's exactly what this visualization is: a peek behind the curtain at
the raw numerical computation powering modern AI.


TECHNICAL NOTES (FOR THE CURIOUS)
----------------------------------
If you're interested in the implementation details:

  • The 3D projection is done with basic trigonometry (cosine and sine
    for rotation, perspective divide for depth). This is how 3D graphics
    engines work before they hand off to the GPU.

  • The numbers are sorted by Z-depth before rendering, so numbers in
    front appear on top of numbers in back. This is called the "painter's
    algorithm" (paint the back first, then paint over it with the front).

  • The flickering is achieved by randomly updating 10% of the values
    every 200ms. A real neural network would have more structured
    updates (driven by input data and backpropagation), but the random
    flickering gives the right feel.

  • The mouse-based rotation maps your cursor position to rotation
    angles: (clientX / width - 0.5) scales your mouse's X position from
    -0.5 to +0.5, then multiplies by π to get an angle in radians.

These techniques are the same ones used in 3D games, data visualization
tools, and scientific simulations. The only difference is that here,
we're visualizing abstract mathematical objects (neural network tensors)
rather than physical objects (characters, terrain, molecules).


GLOSSARY
--------
  Activation    A number that flows through the network when processing
                an input. If you feed the network an image, the pixel
                values turn into activations in the first layer, which
                turn into new activations in the next layer, and so on.

  Backpropagation  The algorithm used to train neural networks. It
                calculates how much each weight should change to reduce
                the loss, and those adjustments flow backward through
                the network (opposite to the forward pass).

  Gradient      A number that tells you how much a weight should change
                during training. Calculated during backpropagation.

  Layer         A slice of the network where all neurons at that level
                are processed together. In this cube, you can think of
                each Z-slice as one layer.

  Matrix        A 2D array of numbers (rows and columns). Neural networks
                are full of matrix multiplications.

  Parameter     Another word for "weight." When you hear "GPT-3 has 175
                billion parameters," it means 175 billion learnable
                numbers.

  Perspective   A method of projecting 3D points onto a 2D screen so
  Projection    that things farther away appear smaller. The formula is:
                screenX = X / Z, screenY = Y / Z (simplified).

  Tensor        A multidimensional array of numbers. A vector is a 1D
                tensor, a matrix is a 2D tensor, and this cube is a 3D
                tensor. Real neural networks use tensors with many more
                dimensions.

  Weight        A learned parameter inside the network. During training,
                weights start random and gradually adjust to solve the
                task.
