LLM PRE-TRAINING: HOW LANGUAGE MODELS LEARN
A Plain-English Guide for Non-Technical Viewers
================================================

WHAT YOU'RE WATCHING
--------------------
You're watching a simplified version of how large language models (like
GPT, BERT, and others) learn from text during pre-training. This is the
first stage of training where the model reads billions of words and
learns to predict missing words based on context.

Nobody is teaching the model what specific words mean or giving it
labeled examples. Instead, the model learns entirely from patterns in
raw text — the same way you might learn a new language by reading books
and filling in blanks.


THE BIG IDEA (30-SECOND VERSION)
---------------------------------
Imagine you're reading a sentence with one word covered up:

  "The quick brown _____ jumps over the lazy dog."

Even though you can't see the hidden word, you can guess it's probably
"fox" or maybe "rabbit" or "cat" — something that jumps. You know this
because you've read thousands of sentences before and learned patterns
about what words go where.

That's exactly what this visualization shows. The model sees a sentence
with one word masked out (shown as [MASK]), tries to guess what belongs
there, checks whether it was right, and adjusts its internal parameters
to get better at guessing next time.

Do this billions of times on billions of sentences, and you get a model
that deeply understands language — even though it was never explicitly
taught grammar, facts, or meanings.


THE TRAINING LOOP (THE FIVE STEPS)
-----------------------------------
The visualization shows a repeating cycle with five stages. These
correspond to the five steps labeled on the screen:

  ① Input: Mask random token
     One word in the sentence is hidden and replaced with [MASK]. The
     model's job is to predict what word was there.

  ② Transformer: Process context
     The model reads all the other words in the sentence (the "context")
     and uses them to build an understanding of what the missing word
     might be. This happens inside the Transformer layers.

  ③ Predict masked token
     Based on the context, the model outputs a probability distribution
     over its entire vocabulary — essentially a guess for every word it
     knows, ranked by likelihood.

  ④ Calculate loss
     The model's prediction is compared to the actual word that was
     masked. The "loss" is a number that measures how wrong the guess
     was. Lower loss = better prediction.

  ⑤ Backprop & update
     The model adjusts its internal weights (parameters) slightly to
     reduce the loss. This is called backpropagation and gradient
     descent — the model learns from its mistake.

Then the cycle repeats with a new masked word. Over many repetitions,
the model gets better and better at predicting masked words.


WHAT YOU'RE SEEING
------------------

The Sentence (top)
  This panel shows the input sentence: "The quick brown fox jumps over
  the lazy dog."

  One word at a time is highlighted in yellow and replaced with [MASK].
  The model doesn't know what word was there — it has to guess based on
  the surrounding words.

  Every few seconds, the mask moves to a different word, and the model
  trains on that position.

The Transformer Model (left, below sentence)
  This box represents the neural network — the "brain" of the language
  model. It contains:

  • Token embeddings (top row of circles)
    Each word is converted into a numerical representation (a vector).
    The masked token is shown in yellow; the others are blue.

  • Self-attention (middle lines)
    The model looks at how each word relates to the others. Lines
    connecting the tokens show attention flow. The masked token "pays
    attention" to all the other words to gather clues about what it
    might be. The red pulsing lines show attention flowing from the
    masked token.

  • Transformer layers (three blue boxes)
    These are the processing stages. Each layer refines the
    representation of each token, mixing information from neighboring
    words via attention and feedforward networks (FFN).

  • Contextual representations (bottom row of circles)
    After processing, each token has a new, context-aware representation.
    The masked token's representation (shown in gold) now contains
    information from the entire sentence. This is what the model uses to
    make its prediction.

Top Predictions for [MASK] (right, top)
  This panel shows the model's top 5 guesses for the masked word, along
  with the probability (confidence) for each guess.

  Early in training, the model's guesses are random and wrong. The true
  word might be ranked 5th or not even in the top 5. The probabilities
  are spread out fairly evenly.

  As training progresses on the same word, you'll see:
    • The true word rises toward the top.
    • Its probability increases (longer bar).
    • Other guesses become less likely (shorter bars).

  After a few training rounds on the same masked position, the model
  learns to predict that word with high confidence.

Loss (Cross-Entropy) (bottom left)
  This panel shows a single number: the loss. Loss measures how far off
  the model's prediction was.

  • High loss (e.g., 3.5) = The model guessed wrong or was very
    uncertain. The true word had low probability.

  • Low loss (e.g., 0.2) = The model guessed correctly with high
    confidence. The true word had probability close to 100%.

  The goal of training is to make the loss go down. Below the loss
  number, there's a sparkline chart showing how the loss has changed
  over time. You'll see it trend downward as the model learns.

  The loss is calculated using cross-entropy, a mathematical formula
  that penalizes wrong guesses heavily. If the model assigns 1%
  probability to the true word, the loss is about 4.6. If it assigns
  99% probability, the loss is about 0.01.

Model Parameters (toy weights) (bottom right)
  This heatmap shows a simplified representation of the model's internal
  parameters — the numbers it adjusts during learning.

  In a real language model, there are billions of these numbers. This
  visualization shows just an 8×8 grid (64 numbers) so you can see them
  update in real time.

  • Red cells = positive weights (the model connects these concepts)
  • Blue cells = negative weights (the model inhibits these connections)
  • Color intensity = strength of the weight

  Watch closely: after each training step, some of the numbers change.
  The model is adjusting its parameters based on the gradient (the
  direction that reduces loss). Over time, the pattern of red and blue
  cells stabilizes as the model learns the structure of language.


HOW THE MODEL LEARNS
---------------------
Here's what's happening under the hood during each training step:

  1. Forward Pass (Steps ① → ③)
     The sentence flows through the model from input to output. The
     tokens get converted to embeddings, processed through attention and
     feedforward layers, and finally produce a prediction for the masked
     word. This is called the "forward pass" because information flows
     forward through the network.

  2. Loss Calculation (Step ④)
     The model compares its prediction to the true word. If the model
     predicted "fox" with 80% probability and the true word was "fox,"
     the loss is low. If it predicted "hen" with 40% probability, the
     loss is high.

  3. Backward Pass (Step ⑤)
     Now the magic happens. The model calculates how each of its
     billions of parameters contributed to the error. It asks: "If I
     change this weight by a tiny amount, will the loss go up or down?"
     This is called backpropagation.

     Then it adjusts every weight slightly in the direction that reduces
     the loss. This is gradient descent — descending the "hill" of error
     by taking small steps downward.

  4. Repeat Millions of Times
     The model sees millions of different sentences with millions of
     different masked words. Gradually, it learns general patterns:
       • Adjectives come before nouns.
       • Verbs need subjects.
       • "The quick brown ____" is probably an animal.
       • "Paris is the capital of ____" is probably "France."

     These patterns aren't explicitly programmed — they emerge from the
     statistics of language.


THE SELF-SUPERVISED LEARNING TRICK
-----------------------------------
Here's the clever part: the model doesn't need labeled data. You don't
need humans to annotate millions of sentences saying "this word should
go here." The training data creates its own labels:

  1. Start with a sentence from the internet: "The quick brown fox..."
  2. Randomly mask one word: "The quick brown [MASK]..."
  3. The model predicts the masked word.
  4. Compare the prediction to the original sentence. You already know
     the answer — it was "fox"!
  5. Update the model to get better at this prediction.

This is called "self-supervised learning" because the supervision signal
comes from the data itself, not from human annotations. It's why we can
pre-train language models on huge amounts of text scraped from the web —
we don't need anyone to manually label it.


WHAT TO WATCH FOR
-----------------
As the visualization runs, look for these patterns:

  Early Training (first few cycles on a word)
    • The top predictions are random and scattered.
    • The true word might not even appear in the top 5.
    • Loss is high (3–5).
    • The weights (bottom right) change dramatically.

  Mid Training (after 2–3 cycles)
    • The true word starts appearing in the top 5.
    • Its probability grows (bar gets longer).
    • Loss decreases (2–3).
    • Weights stabilize somewhat.

  Late Training (final cycle before moving on)
    • The true word jumps to #1 or #2.
    • Its probability is 70–90%.
    • Loss is low (0.5–1.5).
    • Weights change less — the model has learned this pattern.

  Moving to the Next Word
    • After 4 training rounds on the same masked position, the [MASK]
      briefly reveals the true word (so you can see the answer).
    • Then the mask moves to the next word in the sentence.
    • Training starts over on the new word.

Notice that the model doesn't learn perfectly on the first try. It takes
multiple training steps on the same example to adjust its weights
correctly. In real pre-training, the model sees each sentence only once,
but it sees so many similar sentences that the patterns still emerge.


KEY CONCEPTS EXPLAINED
-----------------------

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)
  This specific training method — masking words and predicting them —
  was popularized by BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from
  Transformers). It's one of several pre-training strategies.

  Other models use different methods:
    • GPT uses "next token prediction" (predict the next word, not a
      masked middle word).
    • T5 uses "span corruption" (mask out entire phrases).

  But the core idea is the same: learn from raw text by predicting parts
  of it.

Attention
  The pulsing red lines in the Transformer panel show "self-attention."
  This is how the model figures out which words are relevant to each
  other.

  For example, if the sentence is "The cat sat on the mat," and you mask
  "cat," the model needs to pay attention to "sat" and "mat" to infer
  that the missing word is an animal (things that sit on mats). It also
  attends to "the" to know it's singular.

  Attention is calculated as a weighted average: the model assigns a
  score to each word pair, then mixes their representations proportional
  to the scores. This lets information flow between words.

Cross-Entropy Loss
  This is a standard loss function for classification tasks. Since
  predicting a masked word is classification (pick one word from a
  vocabulary of thousands), cross-entropy makes sense.

  The formula is:
    L = -log(p_true)

  Where p_true is the probability the model assigned to the true word.

    • If p_true = 1.0 (perfect prediction), loss = 0.
    • If p_true = 0.5 (50% sure), loss ≈ 0.69.
    • If p_true = 0.1 (90% wrong), loss ≈ 2.30.
    • If p_true = 0.01 (99% wrong), loss ≈ 4.61.

  The logarithm heavily penalizes low probabilities, which forces the
  model to be confident and correct.

Gradient Descent
  This is the optimization algorithm that updates the model's weights.
  The "gradient" is a mathematical object (technically a vector) that
  points in the direction of steepest increase in loss. Gradient descent
  moves in the opposite direction — downward — to minimize loss.

  The weights don't change all at once. Each update is small (controlled
  by a "learning rate"). You take millions of tiny steps, and
  eventually, the model converges to a good solution.


WHY THIS MATTERS
----------------
Pre-training is the foundation of modern AI language systems. By
learning from massive amounts of text (books, websites, articles), these
models develop a rich understanding of:

  • Grammar and syntax (how sentences are structured)
  • Semantics (what words mean)
  • World knowledge (Paris is in France, water is wet, etc.)
  • Common sense reasoning (birds can fly, rocks can't)

Once pre-trained, the model can be fine-tuned on specific tasks:
  • Question answering
  • Summarization
  • Translation
  • Code generation
  • Chatbot conversations (like ChatGPT)

The pre-training stage does most of the heavy lifting. Fine-tuning is
relatively quick and cheap because the model already "knows" language —
you're just teaching it to apply that knowledge in a specific way.


REAL-WORLD SCALE
----------------
This visualization shows a toy example with:
  • 9 words in the sentence
  • 64 parameters in the weight matrix
  • 17 words in the vocabulary

Real language models are much, much larger:

  • GPT-3: 175 billion parameters, 50,257 vocabulary size, trained on
    300 billion tokens (words).

  • GPT-4: Estimated 1+ trillion parameters.

  • Training takes weeks or months on thousands of GPUs.

  • Training costs millions of dollars in compute resources.

But the fundamental process is the same: mask a word, predict it,
calculate loss, update weights, repeat. Scaled up billions of times.


CONNECTING TO OTHER MODELS
---------------------------
This visualization shows masked language modeling, which is used by:
  • BERT and its variants (RoBERTa, ALBERT, DeBERTa)
  • ELECTRA (a more efficient variant)

Other models use related but different pre-training tasks:

  • GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer): Predicts the next word
    instead of a masked word. Trained to generate text left-to-right.

  • T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer): Treats every task as
    text-to-text (input some text, output some text). Uses span
    corruption (mask out phrases, not just words).

  • BERT is bidirectional (reads context from both sides of the mask).
    GPT is unidirectional (reads context only from the left).

Despite these differences, the core loop is the same:
  1. Give the model some text with part of it missing or obscured.
  2. Make the model predict the missing part.
  3. Calculate loss and update weights.
  4. Repeat on billions of examples.

     Others (like "fox") might take more rounds.


GLOSSARY
--------
  Attention     A mechanism that lets the model focus on relevant words
                when processing a token. Calculated as a weighted average
                based on similarity scores.

  Backprop      Backpropagation. The algorithm that calculates how much
                each weight contributed to the loss, so we know how to
                adjust it.

  BERT          Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers.
                A famous language model trained with masked language
                modeling. Released by Google in 2018.

  Cross-        A loss function for classification. Measures the
  Entropy       difference between predicted probabilities and the true
                label.

  Embedding     A learned numerical representation of a token (word). A
                vector of numbers (e.g., 768 dimensions) that captures
                the meaning and properties of that word.

  Gradient      The direction in which the loss increases most steeply.
  Descent       We move in the opposite direction (descend the gradient)
                to minimize loss.

  Loss          A number that measures how wrong the model's prediction
                was. Lower is better.

  [MASK]        A special token that replaces a word during training. The
                model's job is to predict what word was there.

  MLM           Masked Language Modeling. A pre-training method where
                random words are masked and the model predicts them.

  Parameter     A learnable number inside the model (a weight or bias).
                Large language models have billions of these.

  Pre-training  The first stage of training where the model learns
                general language understanding from massive amounts of
                unlabeled text.

  Self-         Learning from unlabeled data by creating labels
  Supervised    automatically (e.g., masking words and using the original
                text as the "answer").

  Transformer   A neural network architecture that uses attention to
                process sequences (like sentences). The backbone of
                modern language models.

  Token         A unit of text, usually a word or subword. Models process
                text as sequences of tokens.

  Vocabulary    The set of all tokens the model knows. Typically 30,000–
                100,000 tokens for language models.
